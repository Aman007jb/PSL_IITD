We are involved in exploring the laser-plasma interactions using the famous 'Particle-In-Cell (PIC) simulations, understanding the complex dusty plasmas and their dynamics using the Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations, and recently we have taken an interest in looking into the high-energy plasmas Yang-Mills plasma or Quark-Gluon-Plasma (QGP) to name a few.

Laser/EM wave interaction with magnetized plasma

Recent advancements in laser technology, specifically in petawatt (PW) and proposed exawatt (EW) power utilizing chirped pulse amplification (CPA), have revolutionized the field. Enhanced power, repetition rates, and pulse widths allow for diverse applications, from generating GeV electrons to exploring laser-plasma interactions. High-intensity laser beams interacting with plasma yield complex phenomena, including high-energy particle generation, novel radiation sources, and magnetic field manipulation. Magnetized plasma introduces a novel regime, enabling direct laser energy transfer to ions and interaction with low-frequency electromagnetic waves. This holds promise for diverse applications, from space plasma studies to fusion and ion acceleration mechanisms.

Energy absorption mechanisms in Laser Plasma Interaction

Rohit Juneja

PI: Prof. Amita Das

Particle heating is one of the key areas of investigation for laser-plasma interaction studies. The aim of my work is to investigate the mechanisms of energy absorption in the context of laser interactions with unmagnetized and magnetized plasma. These mechanisms include JxB, Vacuum, and Resonance heating. And in the presence of external magnetic field, magnetized resonances (Upper hybrid, Lower hybrid, electron and ion cyclotron resonance) are also possible.

Plasma Based Ion-Accelerators

Plasma being already broken down can sustain very strong electric fields (~10 GV/m as compared to ~10 MV/m in traditional RF accelerators), and therefore is a potential future candidate for efficient charged particle acceleration. Our group's interest lies in exploring proton/ion acceleration through relativistic laser plasma interactions, mainly target normal sheath acceleration (TNSA) scheme involving thick targets. These economically produced energetic proton/ion beams will pave the way for easily accessible cancer therapy among many other applications. However, it has been an area of frontline research for nearly two decades there are still challenges to overcome, pertaining to the spectrum as well as the divergence of the accelerated protons/ions. We employ particle-in-cell (PIC) simulations to investigate the effect of laser as well as target parameters on the quality of the accelerated proton/ion beam.

Molecular Dynamics Simulations

Complex Dusty Plasma

In the expansive field of plasma physics, the study of complex dusty plasmas has emerged as a captivating and multidisciplinary research domain, unlocking new insights into the intricate behavior of matter under extreme conditions. Complex dusty plasmas, often referred to as dusty or dusty-gas plasmas, represent a distinctive state of matter that combines charged particles (plasma) with solid micro- or nano-scale particles, introducing an additional layer of complexity to traditional plasma physics. Dusty plasmas are encountered in various natural and laboratory environments, ranging from interstellar clouds and planetary rings to industrial processing applications. In controlled environment settings that one can delve deeper into understanding the fundamental properties and behaviors of these unique plasmas. In our lab, we simulate these controlled environment using the molecular dynamics simulations for understanding the basic concepts of dusty plasmas such as the transport dynamics, dust-acoustic waves, self-organization, phase transitions, and emergent behavior, etc.

Projects in Complex dusty plasma

Diffusive transport in Strongly coupled dusty plasma in presence of magnetic field

Aman Singh Katariya

PI: Prof. Amita Das

Most of the naturally occuring plasma can be categorised as Dusty plasma, and with increasing potential applications of plasmas, one can easily come across the dusty plasma environment. Many of these applications involve the use of a magnetic field. The dynamics of charged species in a simple plasma is known for a long time. However, with the addition of charged dust species the dynamics are selectively different due to the short range Yukawa potential which is a resultant of debye shielding of dust particles. These dynamics of dust species can also differ with respect to the coupling strength of the Charged particles.
In this project, i focus on understanding the dynamics of the charged dust species in a strongly coupled dusty plasma environment, in presence of an externally applied magnetic field. The study is done by using the molecular dynamics simulations. We use the open-source Large-scale Atomic/Molecular Massively Parallel Simulatori (LAMMPS) Molecular Dynamics Simulator code, developed by developers at Sandia National Labs and Temple University. The simulation results are visualized using the Visual Molecular Dynamics (VMD) software, developed by people from University of Illinois.

UltraCold Plasma

Ultracold plasma research delves into the study of plasmas at extraordinarily low temperatures, typically in the millikelvin or microkelvin range. By maintaining plasmas close to absolute zero, researchers can observe quantum mechanical effects and explore the unique properties that emerge at such extreme cold temperatures. These ultracold plasmas are trapped and controlled using sophisticated techniques involving electromagnetic fields and laser cooling, enabling precise manipulation and examination of individual particles and their interactions. The quantum phenomena exhibited by ultracold plasmas, such as superposition and entanglement, offer a macroscopic platform for studying and harnessing quantum properties.
Beyond its fundamental contributions to quantum mechanics, ultracold plasma research has broad-reaching implications. The field holds promise for advancing quantum information processing, with potential applications in quantum computing and communication technologies. Additionally, insights gained from studying ultracold plasmas contribute to our understanding of astrophysical processes, offering a controlled laboratory setting to explore phenomena relevant to space, such as stellar formation and the behavior of plasmas in cosmic environments. In essence, ultracold plasma research serves as a frontier where the intersection of quantum physics and astrophysics provides new perspectives on fundamental science and paves the way for technological advancements.

Yang-Mills Plasma

The quark-gluon plasma is produced in high-energy heavy ion collisions. It is also believed that the universe went through this phase soon after the big bang. The behavior of this state of matter can be understood by the Yang-Mills theory, a generalization of Maxwell's theory of electrodynamics. The Yang-Mills theory is inherently non-linear due to the nonabelian nature of the gauge transformations, giving rise to new solutions.We study the instabilities arising in the non-abelian plasmas due to counter-streaming beams and bring out the novel unstable modes which are due to the non-abelian effect.

Plasma Driven Space propulsion

Plasma based propulsion has recently become the preferred path for deep space missions, especially due to a much better fuel efficiency than conventional liquid propulsion scheme hence leading to high specific impulse. On top of that, the electrode-less plasma thrusters have additional advantage of considerably longer lifetimes running into several years. There exist mainly two varieties, helicon plasma thruster (with ion cyclotron heating) and electron cyclotron resonance based plasma thruster and both of them rely on the concept of magnetic nozzle. We are interested in developing models and simulate the performance of these electrode-less plasma thrusters, using fluid as well as particle approaches.

Projects in Plasma Propulsion

Study of Plasma Expansion in Presence of Magnetic Field

Subhasis Bag

PI: Prof. Vikrant Saxena

The Electrodeless Plasma Thruster (EPT) stands as an innovative concept within the realm of electric propulsion devices. Unlike conventional solid nozzles, the EPT utilizes a Magnetic Nozzle (MN) to facilitate the acceleration of plasma ions. The MN employs a convergent-divergent magnetic field to convert the internal thermal energy of electrons into directed kinetic energy for ions. This mechanism guides the expanding plasma, generating a substantial thrust. Our focus lies in comprehensively exploring the behavior of the expanding plasma emanating from the Compact Electron Cyclotron Resonance (ECR) Plasma Source (CEPS). The CEPS, developed by the Plasma Physics Laboratory (PPL) at IIT Delhi, relies on a permanent magnet to produce high-density plasma. Notably, the CEPS is distinguished by its portability and flexibility in deployment.
I am engaged in fundamental studies and experimental modeling of expanding magnetized plasma, employing both fluid and particle simulations. This multifaceted approach allows for a comprehensive exploration of the intricate dynamics within the plasma system of CEPS plasma for thruster application.

Error-immune and secure quantum communication with optimal resources

Rajni Bala

PI: Prof. V. Ravishankar

Within the realm of secure quantum communication, we have introduced protocols designed to facilitate task-specific secure communication within layered networks, involving only a single quantum participant. The utilized resources encompass multidimensional entangled/separable states. Furthermore, our approach incorporates contextuality and subspace encoding to achieve secure communication utilizing high-dimensional states, resulting in an enhanced key generation rate in the latter scenario. In the domain of error-mitigation techniques, we have introduced a novel information encoding scheme that enables resilient information transfer without dependence on quantum entanglement. This scheme leverages the randomness inherent in the expectation values of operators, allowing for the identification of invariants that are functions thereof. Additionally, we have identified invariants applicable to orbital angular momentum modes of light traversing weak turbulence.
